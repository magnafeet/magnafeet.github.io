The City of Syracuse, also known as the ‘twenty-minute city’ by its residents, fosters an expansive network of freeways that allows one to drive almost anywhere in the metropolitan area within twenty minutes. According to the Syracuse Transit System Analysis, “this ease of access, combined with the low amounts of traffic congestion has led to a car-centric culture in which public transit cannot compete with the convenience of personal vehicles, even during peak traffic periods. This has led to the marginalization of the transit system by the public, where the majority of riders are transit-dependent (low income, elderly, disabled, and students). The marginalization of the transit system has resulted in a series of service revisions and cutbacks that negatively affect the mobility of the core transit-dependent ridership, further reducing frequency, and increasing travel times. As more-attractive transit options are introduced, parking demand would decrease, resulting in the opportunity for redevelopment of the City into a more-livable, pedestrian-friendly environment with mixed land uses.” [1] 

As previously stated, due to the current limitations of the public transportation system and convenience of personal vehicles, many Syracuse residents prefer to commute by car. However, it is said that one car produces approximately 4.6 tons of carbon dioxide a year, which is equivalent to 0.2 ounces of Co2 a minute. [2]  If over 44,000 vehicles pass through the I-81 each day[3] and assuming that it takes ‘twenty minutes’ for these vehicles to either reach their destination or to pass through Syracuse, these cars will produce roughly  5.5 tons of Co2 in  one day. Adding the vehicles that are driving around the neighborhoods and existing public transportation systems, this figure may actually be closer to the amount of Co2 a single US household produces in a  year.
